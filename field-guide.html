Foundation

What a COA is — and is not

A Certificate of Analysis (COA) is the primary quality assurance document in the research peptide supply chain. It records the analytical results for a specific production batch, performed by an independent third-party laboratory. It is not a marketing claim and not a guarantee of fitness for any particular research application.

Each COA is batch-specific. The numbers on one COA do not apply to any other batch, even of the same compound. Always verify that your vial's lot number matches the COA exactly before proceeding.

Field by field

What each data point actually means

FIELD 01
HPLC Purity
≥ 99% target

High-Performance Liquid Chromatography

HPLC separates a peptide from other UV-absorbing organic impurities and reports the target peptide as a percentage of total UV-absorbing material. A ≥99% result means less than 1% of the UV signal comes from anything other than your target peptide.

Critical limitation: HPLC does not detect water, inorganic salts, trifluoroacetate (TFA) counter-ions, or non-chromophore contaminants. A peptide can be 99% pure by HPLC and yet only 72% net usable peptide by mass. See Field 02.
FIELD 02
Net Peptide Content
≥ 85% typical

Actual usable peptide mass

Net peptide content measures what fraction of the powder's mass is the actual peptide, after accounting for all co-lyophilized material — water (moisture), counter-ions like TFA or acetate, and residual salts. It is determined by a separate assay: amino acid analysis (AAA), elemental analysis, or nitrogen-based methods.

Why it matters for dosing: If a vial is labeled "5 mg" and net peptide content is 80%, the actual peptide mass is 4.0 mg — not 5.0 mg. Quantitative experiments must account for this when preparing stock solutions.
FIELD 03
MS Identity
Confirmed

Mass Spectrometry identity confirmation

Mass spectrometry measures the molecular mass of the compound in the sample. The observed mass is compared against the theoretical mass calculated from the peptide's amino acid sequence. A "Confirmed" result means the observed mass matches theory within the instrument's tolerance — typically ±0.02 Da or better.

MS confirms identity; HPLC confirms purity. Both are required. A high HPLC purity number without MS identity confirmation cannot rule out that the high-purity compound is simply a different peptide with similar chromatographic behavior.
FIELD 04
Batch / Lot No.
Must match vial

Production batch identifier

Every COA is issued for one specific production batch. The lot number on the COA must match the lot number printed on your vial label exactly — character for character. A COA from a different batch provides no analytical assurance for the batch you received.

On arrival: Before opening any vial, compare the lot number on the label against the lot number on the COA header. Photograph both as a lab record. If they differ, contact the supplier before use and do not assume the COA applies.
FIELD 05
Sterility
Pass

Microbial absence testing

Sterility testing confirms the absence of viable microorganisms using membrane filtration or direct inoculation per USP <71> or equivalent. A passing result indicates the tested sample met the acceptance criteria at the time of testing.

Note: Sterility testing is a sample-based statistical assessment, not a guarantee of 100% sterility across all units. Proper aseptic handling during reconstitution is the researcher's responsibility.
FIELD 06
Endotoxin
< 1 EU/mg

Bacterial endotoxin limit

Endotoxins are lipopolysaccharides released from the outer membrane of gram-negative bacteria that can interfere with many in vitro assay systems. The Limulus Amebocyte Lysate (LAL) test quantifies endotoxin levels in EU/mg (endotoxin units per milligram of peptide).

In vitro threshold: <1 EU/mg is the standard specification for research-grade material. Higher levels can produce artifacts in cell-based assays, particularly those sensitive to TLR4 activation or NF-κB signaling pathways.

Critical distinction

HPLC purity vs. net peptide content — not the same number

This is the most commonly misunderstood aspect of peptide COA interpretation. Both numbers must be present; neither alone is sufficient for precise experimental dosing.

HPLC Purity

Peptide vs. peptide impurities

Measures only UV-absorbing organic compounds. Counter-ions, moisture, and inorganic salts are invisible to the detector. A peptide can show 99% HPLC purity while containing 20–30% non-peptide mass by weight.

Net Peptide Content

Peptide vs. everything

Measures the actual peptide fraction of total powder mass. Requires a separate assay (AAA, elemental analysis, or UV spectrophotometry). This is the number to use when calculating molar concentrations for cell-based or binding assays.

Practical example: A 10 mg vial labeled ≥99% HPLC purity with 82% net peptide content contains approximately 8.2 mg of usable peptide. Preparing a 1 mM stock solution requires adjusting the dissolved mass calculation accordingly. Ignoring net content introduces a systematic dosing error of ~18% in this example.

On arrival

Lot matching — verify before you open the vial

A COA from the same compound but a different batch provides zero analytical assurance for the vial in your hands. Batch numbers encode the specific production run, purification sequence, and testing event. They are not interchangeable.

  • Locate the lot/batch number in the COA header — it is typically in the format ABC26-NN-NNN
  • Locate the lot number printed on your vial label or packaging
  • Compare character by character — they must be identical
  • Photograph both the vial label and the COA header as a lab record before opening
  • If numbers do not match, contact your supplier immediately and do not use the material
  • Retain the COA with your lab records for the duration of experiments using that batch
Quality signals

Red flags — signs of a deficient or fraudulent COA

Not all COAs are equal. These patterns indicate documentation that should not be relied upon without independent re-testing.

Missing lot number

A generic COA with no batch number cannot be matched to a specific production run. It may apply to no actual batch.

No chromatogram or raw data

A purity percentage without the accompanying HPLC chromatogram or data file cannot be independently verified.

HPLC purity only — no MS identity

Without mass spectrometry identity confirmation, a high purity number cannot confirm the compound's identity.

In-house lab without accreditation

Testing performed by the seller's own laboratory without ISO 17025 or equivalent third-party accreditation is an inherent conflict of interest.

Reused or recycled COAs

The same COA appearing with different order numbers, dates, or customer information indicates documentation fraud.

Lot number mismatch

The COA lot number differs from the vial label. This is the single most common documentation failure in the supply chain.

Missing net peptide content

A COA reporting only HPLC purity without net peptide content is insufficient for accurate dosing in quantitative research.

Implausible purity (100.0%)

A reported purity of exactly 100% is analytically implausible for any real sample and indicates fabricated data.

Pre-use protocol

Receipt checklist — complete before opening any vial

  • Lot match confirmed — COA batch number matches vial label exactly
  • HPLC purity ≥ 99% — or at minimum meets the specification for your application
  • Net peptide content documented — record the value; use it in all concentration calculations
  • MS identity confirmed — observed mass matches theoretical mass
  • Sterility pass — per applicable pharmacopeial method
  • Endotoxin < 1 EU/mg — or within the limit for your specific assay system
  • Net weight recorded — note any overage or underage from the labeled quantity
  • Storage conditions verified — compound was shipped and stored per COA specifications
  • Issuing lab identified — third-party accredited laboratory named on the document
  • COA retained in lab records — file with experiment logs for the life of the batch
